The Decline

Fire incidents have dropped significantly since 2014. Building regulations and smoke alarms are working. Dwelling fires dominate.

The Rise

Special service now accounts for 41% of all calls. Since 2014, forced entries have grown +127% and agency assists are nearly 10× higher (560 in 2014, 5,427 in 2025). The brigade increasingly handles incidents that are not fires at all.

2.3 The Outward Shift

Across all three incident types, outer London is growing faster than inner London. The indexed chart below (2014 = 100) makes the divergence clear.

Fire: Inner −6.9% vs Outer +6.7%. False Alarm: Inner +26% vs Outer +35%. Special Service: Inner +89% vs Outer +104%.

Outer boroughs are absorbing the bulk of the new demand, yet station coverage there has barely changed since 2014.

03

When Emergencies Strike

Fire services typically plan for a flat demand curve, but the monthly data reveals overlapping seasonal peaks across incident types. Scroll through each one to see when its calls cluster.

3.1 The Seasonal Landscape

Five Seasonal Signals

Each incident type follows its own seasonal rhythm. Scroll to see them one by one — then watch what happens when they collide.

Outdoor Fire

Peaks in July at 9,366 incidents, over 4× the December low of 2,112. Dry weather and vegetation drive the summer surge.

Dwelling Fire

Remarkably flat across the year, varying only between 4,800 and 5,500 per month. Residential fires are a constant, year-round risk.

Flooding

Bimodal: a winter peak in December (8,598) and a smaller summer peak in July (8,440). Storm seasons at both ends of the year overlap with the outdoor fire surge.

Forced Entry

Highest in December (9,774), with a secondary summer peak (9,721 in July). The amplitude is modest, around 30% between low and high months.

Agency Assist

After a February dip, the trend rises through the year and peaks in December at 2,503, 41% above the February low. Inter-agency demand concentrates in winter.

The Temporal Collision

July stacks an outdoor fire surge on top of summer flooding while forced entries stay near their annual peak. December delivers the same pile-up: flooding, forced entries, and agency assists all peak together. A flat staffing model has no slack for either month.

Reading the City

London Fire Brigade answers over 100,000 calls a year. Fewer than 1 in 7 are actual fires.

The rest, floods, forced entries, agency assists, are signals of a city ageing, warming, and stretching its safety nets. Since the 2014 station closures, new demand has shifted outward to where response is slowest, and seasonal peaks in July and December stack incident types on top of one another.

Urban Analytics lets us read these signals and ask: is the system resilient enough for the city London is becoming?

Methodology

Analysis of roughly 1.34 million LFB incident records (2014 to 2025) from the London Datastore (OGL v2). Spatial analysis uses 250m grid aggregation with pyproj BNG-to-WGS84 coordinate transformation. The deprivation and fire correlation is computed on the 2018 to 2023 subset because ward boundaries were redrawn in 2014, and the 2018 to 2023 file uses the post-revision ONS ward codes that match IMD 2019. Correlations use Pearson's r on ward-level fire rates per 1,000 population, joined to IMD 2019 (MHCLG) via ward code with name-based fallback. Ward-level IMD scores should be read as 2019 estimates rather than current values.

AI tools used

Claude (Anthropic) and ChatGPT (OpenAI) were used to scaffold the scrollytelling structure, draft initial Chart.js / D3 / Mapbox boilerplate, and review code for bugs. The data preparation scripts (prepare_data.py, prepare_grids_v3.py, imd_analysis.py) were drafted with AI assistance and reviewed by the team. All analytical claims in the text (Pearson r, quartile gaps, growth rates, monthly peaks, borough totals) were computed directly from LFB data and cross-checked against the source files.
