What They Respond To

London Fire Brigade answers over 100,000 calls a year. The composition of those calls has fundamentally shifted since 2014 - fires are declining, but new demands are surging. This chapter maps what, where, and how much.

Three Trajectories

The Wasted Effort

75% of false alarms are automatic fire alarms malfunctioning. Nearly 1 in 2 calls is a wasted trip.

The Decline

Fire incidents have dropped significantly since 2014. Building regulations and smoke alarms are working. Dwelling fires dominate.

The Rise

Special service now accounts for 41% of all calls. Forced entries +86%, agency assists +433%. The fire brigade is becoming a multi-purpose emergency service.

Spatial Distribution

Three incident types, three completely different spatial patterns. 250m grid aggregation reveals sub-borough detail invisible at coarser scales (cf. Corcoran et al., 2007).

The Danger Zone

Boroughs where special service demand is growing fastest are often those where the fire service takes longest to arrive.

The Outward Shift

Across all three incident types, outer London is growing faster than inner London. The indexed chart below (2014 = 100) makes the divergence clear.

Fire: Inner -6.9% vs Outer +6.7%. False Alarm: Inner +26% vs Outer +35%. Special Service: Inner +89% vs Outer +104%.

The fire service was built for a centralised city. London's emergency demand is decentralising - but the stations haven't moved.

Deprivation and Fire Risk

The poorest quarter of wards has 64% higher fire rates than the richest.

Yet deprived areas get faster responses - because they sit in inner London where stations are densest. The inequality is not in who gets rescued, but in who needs rescuing.

03

When Emergencies Strike

Fire services typically plan for a flat demand curve. But the data reveals multiple overlapping seasonal peaks - a temporal collision that challenges resource planning.

The Seasonal Landscape

Outdoor Fire

Peaks in July at nearly 4x winter levels. Dry weather and vegetation drive a dramatic summer surge.

Dwelling Fire

Remarkably flat across the year. No clear seasonal pattern - residential fires are a constant, year-round risk.

Flooding

Summer storms drive a July-August spike - coinciding with the outdoor fire peak. Two crises competing for the same resources.

Forced Entry

Does it peak in winter, when the elderly are most vulnerable to falls and isolation? Or is it flat like dwelling fires?

Agency Assist

When do other services call the fire brigade for help? The seasonal pattern reveals the rhythm of inter-agency demand.

The Temporal Collision

July demands surge capacity for outdoor fires AND flooding simultaneously - while forced entries and agency assists maintain baseline pressure. Resource planning must account for these overlapping peaks.

Reading the City

London Fire Brigade answers over 100,000 calls a year. Fewer than 1 in 7 are actual fires.

The rest - floods, forced entries, agency assists - are signals of a city ageing, warming, and stretching its safety nets. Since the 2014 station closures, new demand has shifted outward to where response is slowest, and seasonal peaks create resource collisions that a flat staffing model cannot absorb.

Urban Analytics lets us read these signals and ask: is the system resilient enough for the city London is becoming?

Methodology

Analysis of 1.1M+ LFB incident records (2014-2025) from London Datastore (OGL v2). Spatial analysis uses 250m grid aggregation with pyproj BNG-to-WGS84 coordinate transformation. Ward-level IMD 2019 from MHCLG. Statistical correlations via Pearson test. IMD 2019 is used as the deprivation measure; while deprivation patterns shift over time, the broad spatial structure of London's inequality has remained relatively stable over the study period.
